GILTI Is Dead, Long Live NCTI: What US Owners of Singapore Companies Owe in 2026

📅 May 2026 ⏱ 11 min read ✍️ Karman Editorial Team

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), signed July 4, 2025 and effective for tax years beginning after December 31, 2025, replaced GILTI with NCTI - and the math is meaningfully worse for US persons who own Singapore companies. If you're a US citizen, green card holder, or US tax resident with 10%+ ownership of a Singapore Pte Ltd that meets the CFC test, your annual US tax inclusion roughly doubles in 2026 versus 2025. This piece walks through what changed, who it hits, a worked example for a typical Singapore Pte Ltd, and what to do about it.

We assume basic familiarity with Subpart F and CFC concepts. If you don't know whether your Singapore Pte Ltd is a CFC, the short answer is: if more than 50% of the shares (by vote or value) are held by US shareholders who each own 10%+, it's a CFC. Most US-founded Singapore Pte Ltds are.

What OBBBA actually changed

GILTI ("Global Intangible Low-Taxed Income") was introduced in the 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act to discourage US multinationals from parking IP in low-tax jurisdictions. OBBBA renamed and rewrote it as NCTI ("Net CFC Tested Income") with three big substantive changes:

ElementGILTI (through 2025)NCTI (from 2026)
QBAI deduction10% return on tangible assets excluded from inclusionEliminated - 100% of CFC tested income is in scope
Section 250 deduction50% of inclusion deductible40% deductible (effective US rate rises from 10.5% to ~12.6%)
Foreign Tax Credit haircut20% of foreign tax was disallowed10% haircut (this is the one piece of good news)
FDII / FDDEI rate13.125%~14% (renamed FDDEI - Foreign-Derived Deduction Eligible Income)
Inclusion yearTax years beginning after Dec 31, 2017Tax years beginning after Dec 31, 2025

The QBAI elimination is the killer for asset-light businesses. A Singapore SaaS or consulting Pte Ltd with no real tangible assets used to get a small reduction in the GILTI inclusion via QBAI; now they get nothing.

Who this hits

NCTI affects:

  • US persons owning 10%+ of a Singapore Pte Ltd that is a CFC. This includes US citizens abroad, green card holders living in Singapore, and accidental Americans.
  • US LLCs that own a Singapore Pte Ltd. The inclusion flows through to the US member.
  • US C-Corps with Singapore subsidiaries. The inclusion is a corporate-level item, generally less painful because of the corporate FTC mechanics.

NCTI does NOT directly affect:

  • Non-US shareholders of a Singapore Pte Ltd (Singapore residents, Indian residents, etc.)
  • US persons owning less than 10% of a CFC (you escape NCTI but may still face PFIC if the entity is one)
  • Singapore Pte Ltds that are not CFCs (i.e., majority owned by non-US persons)

Worked example: US founder, US$220K Singapore Pte Ltd profit

Let's run the numbers for a realistic scenario: a US-citizen founder living in Singapore on an EP, owning 100% of a Singapore Pte Ltd that earns S$300K (~US$220K) of operating profit in 2026. The business is asset-light (think SaaS, consulting, agency).

Step 1: Singapore-side tax
  • SUTE applies: first S$100K is 75% exempt (S$25K taxable), next S$100K is 50% exempt (S$50K taxable), rest fully taxable (S$100K)
  • Chargeable income: S$175K. Tax at 17%: S$29,750
  • 40% Budget 2026 CIT rebate: -S$11,900
  • Singapore tax: S$17,850 (~US$13,200)
Step 2: NCTI inclusion (as the US shareholder)
  • Tested income (Singapore profits in USD): US$220,000
  • Less Singapore tax allocated: -US$13,200
  • Less QBAI return: $0 (eliminated under OBBBA)
  • Net Tested Income (NCTI): US$206,800
Step 3: US tax on NCTI
  • Section 250 deduction: 40% x $206,800 = -$82,720
  • Taxable NCTI: $124,080
  • If you take the inclusion at individual rates (37% top bracket): ~$45,910 US tax
  • If you elect Section 962 (corporate rates 21%): ~$26,057 US tax
Step 4: Foreign Tax Credit
  • Singapore tax paid: $13,200
  • 10% haircut under NCTI rules: $13,200 x 90% = $11,880 creditable
  • Net US tax (Section 962 election): $26,057 - $11,880 = ~$14,177
  • Net US tax (no Section 962): $45,910 - $11,880 = ~$34,030
Step 5: Total combined tax
  • Singapore: $13,200
  • US (Section 962 elected): $14,177
  • Combined: $27,377 on $220,000 profit (12.4% effective rate)
  • Without Section 962: $47,230 (21.5% effective rate - similar to Delaware C-Corp)

Compare to GILTI 2025: Same scenario under the old rules would have produced roughly $20K-22K combined tax (the QBAI deduction would have shaved off the inclusion further, and the Section 250 deduction at 50% would have been more generous). NCTI raises the bill by approximately 25-35%.

Section 962 election: usually a winning move

The Section 962 election is the single most useful planning lever for individual US shareholders of a Singapore Pte Ltd. It lets you elect to be taxed on Subpart F and NCTI inclusions at corporate rates (21%) instead of individual rates (up to 37%) and claim the foreign tax credit normally. Without it, the inclusion is taxed at ordinary individual rates and the FTC mechanics are clunky.

The catch: If you later distribute the previously-taxed earnings to yourself as a dividend, the dividend is taxable at individual rates with a basis recovery for the corporate-level tax already paid. Net effect: you defer second-layer tax until distribution, but you pay it eventually.

When Section 962 makes sense: You plan to leave profits in the Singapore Pte Ltd (reinvest in the business, build retained earnings). You don't need the cash personally. You're in a high US individual tax bracket.

When it doesn't: You strip every dollar out as soon as it's earned. Then there's no deferral benefit and the second-layer tax bite reduces the value.

Other planning options

Check-the-box (CTB) election to disregard the Singapore Pte Ltd.
  • You file Form 8832 electing to treat the Pte Ltd as a disregarded entity for US tax purposes (since it's a single-member entity, this is available)
  • Effect: Singapore profits flow directly to your US 1040 as if you earned them personally - taxed at individual rates without the CFC machinery
  • FTC for Singapore tax is still available, more cleanly than under NCTI
  • You completely avoid Form 5471 (replaced by Form 8858 for foreign disregarded entities)
  • Best for: Solo founders who repatriate everything and want simplicity
  • Worst for: Founders who want to retain earnings in Singapore - CTB election eliminates the deferral that NCTI permits
Hold the Singapore Pte Ltd through a US C-Corp.
  • You set up a Delaware C-Corp parent that owns 100% of the Singapore Pte Ltd
  • The C-Corp is the US shareholder for CFC purposes; NCTI inclusion happens at the C-Corp level (21% corporate rate, cleaner FTC)
  • Effective NCTI rate at the corporate level: ~12.6% (close to break-even with Singapore tax for many fact patterns)
  • The catch: dividends from the Singapore Pte Ltd to the C-Corp are tax-free (Section 245A participation exemption), but distributions from the C-Corp to you as the individual shareholder are taxable as qualified dividends (15-23.8%)
  • Best for: Founders who anticipate US fundraising or want to optimize for US-LP investor compatibility
Restructure ownership so the Singapore Pte Ltd is no longer a CFC.
  • If you bring in non-US co-founders or investors who collectively own more than 50%, the Pte Ltd ceases to be a CFC
  • NCTI no longer applies (though you may face PFIC if the entity has >75% passive income)
  • Practical only if you legitimately need outside ownership and the dilution makes business sense

What you must file (compliance burden in 2026)

  • Form 5471 - annually. Categories 1, 4, or 5 depending on your ownership and CFC status. Schedules I-1 (NCTI), J (E&P), P, Q, R all in scope. The IRS has automated $10K penalty enforcement for late/missing 5471s in 2026 - more on that in our Form 5471 filing guide.
  • Form 8992 - calculates your NCTI (formerly GILTI). One per US shareholder.
  • Form 8993 - if you elect Section 250 deduction (you should).
  • Form 1118 (corporate) or 1116 (individual) - foreign tax credit calculation.
  • Form 8832 - if you make a check-the-box election. One-time filing.
  • FBAR (FinCEN 114) - if your Singapore Pte Ltd's bank accounts cross US$10K aggregate.
  • Form 8938 - if your foreign financial assets exceed the threshold (varies by filing status and residence). See our FBAR + 8938 checklist.

What you should do this quarter

  1. Confirm whether your Singapore Pte Ltd is a CFC for the 2026 tax year (US ownership, by vote AND by value, more than 50%)
  2. Run the NCTI numbers with your CPA for projected 2026 profits - if you've been getting away with $0 GILTI inclusions historically due to QBAI, you'll likely have a real bill in 2026
  3. Decide whether to elect Section 962 (if individual shareholder) - generally yes if you're keeping cash in the Pte Ltd
  4. Consider whether check-the-box or a US C-Corp holdco changes the math favorably for your facts
  5. Make sure your 2026 estimated tax payments reflect the higher inclusion - the IRS will charge underpayment interest if you wait until April 2027
